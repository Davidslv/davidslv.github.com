<h1 id="about-the-author-unnumbered" class="unnumbered">About the Author</h1>

<p>David Silva is a software engineer with over fifteen years building production<br />
systems. His work has spanned public-sector services at GOV.UK and the UK<br />
Ministry of Justice, fintech platforms at Creditspring and Tembo Money, and<br />
large-scale recruitment infrastructure at Indeed.</p>

<p>Vanilla Roguelike, the codebase this book is built on, took five years to<br />
reach the form it has today. It started as a few hundred lines of Ruby<br />
exploring maze algorithms and grew, through several breaking points, into a<br />
complete game with proper Entity-Component-System architecture, event-driven<br />
logging, and a test suite you can actually trust. The chapters here follow<br />
that journey honestly: the architecture problem in Chapter 8 was a real<br />
breaking point in the codebase, the move to ECS was a real refactor, and the<br />
testing patterns in Chapter 19 are the ones that held up under change. He<br />
wrote this book because he could not find one that explained those lessons in<br />
working Ruby code, with the trade-offs on the page rather than hidden behind<br />
framework conventions.</p>

<p>His other book, <em>Modular Rails: Architecture for the Long Game</em>, applies the<br />
same thinking to large Rails monoliths — using mountable engines to decompose<br />
them without reaching for microservices. Where this book argues for clean<br />
architecture in a small Ruby codebase, <em>Modular Rails</em> argues for it in a<br />
large one.</p>
